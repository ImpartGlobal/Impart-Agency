# Brand Benchmark

A yardstick document for the Impart Agency brand guide. Read this first, sanity check it, then we write `docs/brand-guide.md` against it.

The target is clear: Impart's brand system should sit in the top tier of agency and tech brand work globally. That means the system has to hold up next to Stripe, Linear, QuantumBlack, Koto, and Pentagram. It should also answer specific questions those references leave open, because Impart is a smaller, younger, South African AI agency with different trust obligations and a tighter point of view.

This benchmark is organised in five parts:

1. The three reference pools I read, and the specific thing each one teaches.
2. What the world-class guides all share (table stakes).
3. Where the best few actually push further (the differentiators).
4. Where most guides go soft (the traps to avoid).
5. The structure Impart's guide should follow to clear the bar.

---

## 1. The reference pools

### Pool A. AI and consulting competitors

These are the firms Impart is measured against in a procurement shortlist or a C-suite conversation. Their public brand work tells you how the category presents itself, and where there is room for Impart to look sharper.

**QuantumBlack, AI by McKinsey.** The clearest lesson here is earned authority through one confident visual move. When QuantumBlack refreshed its identity, it commissioned the AI artist Sougwen Chung to create a painting that captured the hybrid-intelligence idea, and used that work as the seed of the new visual system. The visual language is specific, conceptual, and credentialled. It doesn't rely on stock AI tropes (neural-net graphics, generic blue gradients). For Impart, the lesson is that one strong visual idea, executed with conviction and tied back to a clear position, beats a grab-bag of on-trend elements.

**BCG X, Thoughtworks, Slalom, Fractal, Palantir.** Across this pool, the shared trait is editorial confidence. Long-form thinking is treated as a first-class asset, with typography and layout given room to operate at a serious scale. Palantir in particular demonstrates that a technical brand can run on black, one accent, and one display face, and still feel like the most specialist option in the room. Impart should read that and notice what is missing from its current site: editorial density, long-form thinking pieces, and typography that signals rigour.

### Pool B. Tech companies with world-class public design systems

These are the references for how a brand system is documented, versioned, and actually used day to day. They are the benchmarks for the brand guide as a working document.

**Linear.** The standout pattern at Linear is opinionated restraint. The identity runs on a small, fixed palette (including a signature desaturated blue), a clear rule that "Linear" is always capitalised as a proper noun, and a lot of breathing room. The Linear Method companion document extends the identity into a set of working principles about software and teams. The takeaway for Impart: a brand guide earns authority when it takes positions and refuses to hedge. Linear says no to flexible workflows and no to busy work. Those refusals are the brand.

**Stripe.** The template for "quiet precision at scale." Stripe's documentation, product surface, and marketing all share a restrained palette, a specific information-dense layout grammar, and a way of writing that treats the reader as technically capable. The lesson for Impart is not to copy the aesthetic (every fintech has tried that and lost), but to match the level of craft in the writing: sentences that do work, no decorative padding, no trying to sound friendly.

**Airbnb, Figma, Vercel, Mailchimp, Shopify Polaris.** The lesson across this group is that a brand guide is really three documents stacked together: a visual identity, a content style guide, and a design system. Mailchimp's Content Style Guide is the clearest public example of the middle piece. It separates voice (constant) from tone (variable by context), gives the voice four adjectives with behavioural rules, and illustrates tone shifts with side-by-side examples. Shopify Polaris layers a full product-system layer on top of that. Impart's guide needs all three strata. Most agency brand guides skip the second one and end up feeling like decoration.

### Pool C. Creative agencies with the sharpest voice work

These references teach how an agency presents itself as a maker of brands. Impart is a service business, so the self-presentation has to carry weight.

**Koto.** In 2025 Koto shipped major identity work for Amazon (a 15-market, 50-sub-brand modernisation, including the custom alphabet Ember Modern), Tripadvisor (including the custom Trip Sans typeface), and Lyft. The Koto house brand is a masterclass in confident minimalism: a small set of type decisions, generous white space, and case-study pages that read like short essays with strong supporting imagery. For Impart, the lesson is that a case-study page is a core brand asset. It should feel as considered as the homepage.

**Pentagram.** The entire Pentagram site is case-study first. There is no long About page, no service list up front, no pricing table. The work itself is the argument. Impart is not ready to run a Pentagram-style site because the work library is still being built, but the principle holds: the long-term brand is the portfolio, and the brand guide should anticipate that shift.

**R/GA, Wieden+Kennedy, IDEO, Huge.** Each of these has an earlier, distinct voice, but the useful reference now is how they present thinking. Long-form essays, point-of-view documents, and clear editorial programs. These are not blog content farms. They are curated, infrequently updated, and punch above their weight. Impart's content program should be structured the same way from day one.

## 2. What the world-class guides all share (the table stakes)

Any brand guide that claims to be in the top tier does all of the following. If Impart's guide is missing any of these, it is below the line.

**A single-line positioning statement that is specific.** Not "we help brands grow" territory. Linear: high-quality software, opinionated. Stripe: infrastructure for internet commerce. QuantumBlack: AI by McKinsey. The statement is testable. You can decide whether a piece of work fits it or doesn't.

**A small, memorable palette with clear roles.** Usually one bg, one surface, one accent, one ink, and a neutral scale. Every great brand is recognisable from its palette alone. Roles matter as much as hex values: which colour is CTA, which is brand, which is neutral, which is error.

**A short type system.** One display face and one body face is the standard. Sometimes a third utility face (often mono) for code or data. No more than three weights actually used in production.

**Voice defined by a small number of adjectives with behavioural rules.** Mailchimp names four: plainspoken, genuine, translator, dry humour. Each adjective comes with do and don't examples. The adjectives have to be specific enough to be disputable. "Friendly, helpful, trustworthy" does not count.

**A clear treatment of the logo, with rules and with examples of wrong.** Lockup, clear space, minimum size, on light, on dark, single-colour, monogram, and do-not-do grid. The do-not-do grid is often where weak guides fall over because they don't commit.

**Motion and interaction principles.** Animation timing, easing, how elements enter and exit, and the rule for when motion is appropriate. Most agency guides skip this. Top-tier product brands do not.

**A writing style guide that covers product copy, marketing copy, and long-form.** Word choice, capitalisation, numbers, dates, punctuation, the list of words the brand uses and the list it refuses. Shopify Polaris is a public benchmark here.

**A layout system that goes beyond the grid.** Containers, spacing scale, vertical rhythm, how density is modulated across surfaces. Sections on a long marketing page vs a product surface vs a dashboard should all feel related and obey the same spacing logic.

**Imagery direction with specific art-direction rules.** Exposure, colour grade, subject framing, what to avoid. A line like "use authentic imagery" fails as guidance because it tells no one what to do. A line like "shots are warm-dark-graded, subject off-centre to the right, negative space reserved on the left for headline" is the standard to hit.

**Case study presentation rules.** How a piece of work is introduced, how results are cited, how the writing is structured. Often missing from agency guides and always present in the great ones.

## 3. Where the best push further (the differentiators)

The table-stakes list keeps a guide out of the "below average" bucket. To clear the top tier, the guide has to do one or more of the following things that most competitors still don't.

**Tie the brand system to a point of view about the work.** Linear's Method is the best public example. The brand functions as the explanation of why the product is the way it is, and it carries more weight than any surface-level styling. Impart's brand guide should contain a short, opinionated doctrine about how AI work should be done: senior practitioners, short cycles, POPIA by default, outcomes over prototypes. This doctrine is the spine of the brand and the case studies and the service pages all hang off it.

**Make the voice testable.** Strong voice guides include "this is us" and "this is not us" side by side, using copy that could plausibly have been written for this brand. Mailchimp does this for tone shifts, Gov.uk for plain language. Impart's voice section should include rewritten before-and-after pairs, using the current site copy as the before.

**Treat long-form typography as an asset class.** The best brands (Stripe, Linear, QuantumBlack, Palantir) invest serious thinking in how articles, reports, and case studies look. Typography, drop caps, pull-quotes, inline figures, footnotes, captions. Impart's guide should specify an editorial type system separate from the marketing type system.

**Publish the guide as living documentation, not a PDF.** Shopify Polaris and Linear's brand docs are web pages with versioning. The guide lives in the repo (`docs/brand-guide.md` and supporting files). It gets updated when the brand moves. Everyone on the team reads from the same source.

**Treat motion as part of the voice.** The rhythm of micro-interactions (hover, scroll reveal, section transitions) is part of how the brand feels. A brand that describes itself as "senior" and "considered" cannot ship bouncy, playful animations without breaking its own rule. The guide should name a motion personality in the same breath as the tone personality.

**Write the guide itself in the voice it describes.** This is the most brutal filter. If the voice section says "plainspoken, no jargon" and the rest of the document uses phrases like "leverage" and "synergise", the brand has already failed the test. Impart's guide has to pass its own voice rules sentence by sentence.

## 4. Where most guides go soft (the traps to avoid)

These failure modes show up even in guides that look nice on the surface. The Impart guide should be read against this list before it ships.

**Generic adjectives.** "Innovative, trusted, human, bold." These mean nothing because every brand claims them. Voice adjectives have to be specific enough that a competitor would not use them.

**Inspiration imagery that is not art direction.** A Pinterest board with the word "vibe" on it is not a rule. The imagery section has to be rule-based, with specific parameters.

**Colour palettes with too many accents.** Two accents is sometimes right. More than that is almost always wrong. Most brand guides ship with four to six accents and end up diluting the system.

**Typography systems with too many weights.** Six weights of a display face look good on the guide page and ruin consistency in production. Three weights used with discipline outperforms six weights used loosely.

**Voice sections without examples.** Adjectives without copy are aspirations. Adjectives with rewritten examples are instructions.

**No rules for failure cases.** What does an empty state sound like? What does an error message sound like? What does a refund email sound like? The best voice guides answer these.

**No pricing or commercial language rules.** When a brand like Impart hides pricing and uses "request a proposal", the guide has to specify how the proposal email is written, how the scoping call is introduced, and how the contract language sounds. Otherwise the brand collapses at the commercial edge.

**Case study prose written like LinkedIn captions.** "We are so excited to announce..." kills the brand faster than any visual choice. The case-study section of the guide should specify structure, sentence length, and the tone of results.

**Guides that are decorative rather than operational.** If a brand guide is a beautiful 60-page PDF that no one has open in another tab while they work, it is decoration. The test is whether a designer or writer actually reads the guide while making something.

**Hedging.** "We sometimes use X, but we also like Y." A brand guide that hedges has no opinion, and therefore no authority. Linear's guide is a good antidote. It says what Linear is and refuses to say what it isn't.

## 5. What Impart's guide has to do to clear the bar

The guide should be a working document with real positions, enforced at the sentence level, and operationalised across writing, visual, motion, and commercial edges. The following structure is the floor. We may add sections but we do not remove them.

### 5.1 Doctrine

A one-page statement of what Impart is, who it serves, what it refuses to do, and what it guarantees. This is the Linear-Method equivalent. It is read before every kickoff, pitch, and hire.

### 5.2 Positioning and audience

A single-sentence position. One paragraph on the buyer profile. One paragraph on the poor-fit profile (who we turn away and why). Specific enough that the team can run the test on inbound leads without a meeting.

### 5.3 Voice

Four to five adjectives, each with:
* a one-line definition
* three do examples (real sentences for this brand)
* three don't examples (the rejected phrasings from the same brief)

Plus a list of words Impart uses and words Impart refuses to use (an explicit vocabulary and anti-vocabulary). And one section on the AI tells to avoid in every piece of copy: em dashes, contrastive "not X, but Y" constructions, pitch-deck cadence, hedging adverbs.

### 5.4 Tone shifts

How voice changes by surface. Homepage hero, service page, case study intro, proposal email, error message, legal page, social post. Each with a side-by-side example.

### 5.5 Naming and language

Capitalisation of "Impart" and "Impart Agency." Product and service names. Currency conventions (ZAR, South African English spelling). Date and number formatting. The 2KO affiliation phrasing.

### 5.6 Logo and mark

Lockup, clear space, minimum size, on dark, on light, monogram, favicon. Do-not-do grid with eight failure cases.

### 5.7 Colour

One background, one surface, one elevated surface, one primary brand, one secondary brand, one ink, one subtle ink, a functional scale (success, warning, error, info). Roles named explicitly. Gradient rules (one named gradient, with stops and angle).

### 5.8 Typography

Display face, body face, optional mono face. Three weights maximum each. Type scale as a named system (display xl, display lg, h1 through h4, body lg, body, small, micro). Rules for line height, tracking, and paragraph measure.

### 5.9 Layout and spacing

Grid, container widths, spacing scale (4px base), section rhythm on marketing pages, density rules on product surfaces. How to break the grid and when.

### 5.10 Motion

Motion personality (e.g. considered, weighty, deliberate). Timing and easing defaults. Rules for scroll reveal, hover, page transitions. Explicit bans on motion styles that break the voice.

### 5.11 Imagery and art direction

Photography grade. Subject framing. Negative-space rules. Illustration approach, if any. Generated imagery rules (what is allowed, what is forbidden, how it is captioned). Envato pull criteria translated into specific parameters.

### 5.12 Long-form editorial

Article page layout, drop cap, pull quote, figure, caption, footnote. Reading typography. Article structure (lede, claim, evidence, implication, close).

### 5.13 Case studies

Structure for every case study: headline, result line, client sentence, problem, approach, what we built, outcome. Tone rules for the results section (no superlatives, specific numbers, no "we are thrilled").

### 5.14 Forms, CTAs, and commercial copy

Every CTA phrasing. Every form field label. Budget range copy. Proposal-email template. Discovery-call intro. Every place the brand touches money, approval, or commitment.

### 5.15 Empty states, errors, loading

The unglamorous surfaces where bad brands die. Specific copy for the common failure cases.

### 5.16 Social, external surfaces

How Impart writes on LinkedIn. How a case-study announcement is phrased. How a hire is announced. How the 2KO affiliation is referenced.

### 5.17 Governance

How the guide is maintained, who owns it, how updates are proposed and approved. The version history, at the top.

## 6. Voice rules that apply to this document and the guide itself

Both `docs/brand-benchmark.md` and `docs/brand-guide.md` have to pass their own tests. The rules below apply to every sentence in both documents, and to every piece of copy on the Impart site going forward.

**Punctuation.** No em dashes anywhere. Hyphens only inside genuine compound words. Use parentheses, colons, semicolons, or full stops to break rhythm.

**Sentence shape.** Avoid the "not X, but Y" structure. Avoid "it's not just A; it's B." Avoid three-item parallel lists as a default cadence. Vary sentence length on purpose, with the longer sentences earning their length.

**Vocabulary.** Refused words: leverage, unlock, empower, seamless, best-in-class, world-class (applied to ourselves), synergy, robust, cutting-edge, revolutionary, game-changing, ecosystem (unless technically accurate), solutions (as a generic product noun), bespoke (overused in South African agency copy).

**Cadence.** No pitch-deck cadence. No short punchy sentences stacked for rhythm. No all-lowercase "vibe" voice. No emoji in body copy.

**Claims.** No invented metrics. No adjectives substituting for evidence. When a claim is made, it is backed by a number, a name, or a specific example. If there is nothing to back it, the sentence is cut.

**Hedging.** No "perhaps," "arguably," "some might say," "we believe that." If we believe it, say it. If we don't, cut it.

**Tense and voice.** Active voice by default. Present tense by default. First person plural ("we") for the agency. Second person ("you") for the reader. Third person avoided unless referring to a specific named client.

## 7. What happens next

Once this benchmark clears Liam's sanity check, the guide itself (`docs/brand-guide.md`) gets written against this structure, in the voice it prescribes. After the guide is written, the existing audit copy in `docs/audit.md` (iteration 2, section 15) gets a clean-up pass against the same voice rules, because the iteration-2 drafts still contain em dashes and contrastive parallel constructions that the new voice refuses. The visual direction file (`docs/visual-direction.html`) expands to match the visual sections of the guide, with reference pulls from Envato slotted in as they land.

The bar is set deliberately high. The measurable test is whether a designer or writer from Koto, Linear, or QuantumBlack would read our guide, find it operational rather than decorative, and recognise it as a peer document. If it reads that way, we are in the top tier. If it reads like most agency brand guides, we missed.
